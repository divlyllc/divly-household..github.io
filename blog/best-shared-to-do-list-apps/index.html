The best shared to-do list apps for couples in 2026

By the Divly founders · Published June 9, 2026 · Updated July 16, 2026

The best shared to-do list app for couples keeps every task in one place both of you can see, lets you give each task a clear owner, handles recurring chores automatically, and syncs in real time on iOS and Android. General productivity apps can work, but tools built for a household tend to fit the way two people actually run a home. Below is what to look for and how the popular options compare.

What makes a to-do list app good for couples?

A personal task manager and a shared household app look similar but solve different problems. For two people sharing a home, the features that matter most are:

  • Genuinely shared lists. Both partners can add, edit and complete items and see changes instantly, so the list is not something one person maintains for the other.
  • Clear ownership per task. Each item can be assigned to one person, which is what stops chores from falling into the gap between "someone will do it" and nobody doing it.
  • Recurring tasks and reminders. The bins go out on the same night every week. The app should remember that so neither of you has to.
  • Cross-platform sync. One of you on iPhone, the other on Android, both seeing the same picture.
  • Low friction. If it takes more effort to log a task than to just do it, the app gets abandoned within a week.

If you are still deciding how to divide the work in the first place, our guide to splitting chores with your partner pairs well with any of these tools.

Which shared to-do list apps are worth considering?

There is no single winner for every couple, so it helps to match the tool to how much you want it to handle. A few widely used options:

  • Cozi is built around family organization, with a shared calendar and shared lists in one place. It suits households that want the calendar and the lists living together.
  • Todoist is a flexible task manager with shared projects, recurring due dates and assignees. It is a strong pick if you also want a serious personal task system alongside the shared one.
  • TickTick covers similar ground to Todoist and folds in a built-in calendar and habit tracking, which is handy if you want tasks and routines in the same app.
  • Google Keep offers shared checklists that are quick and free, though it stays deliberately simple and does not do much with ownership or recurrence.
  • Any.do combines tasks, lists and a calendar with shared spaces, aimed at people who want a clean, everyday planner.

General-purpose apps like these can absolutely work for a couple. The trade-off is that most are designed for one person first and sharing second, so the household setup and the sense of shared ownership are things you assemble yourself.
