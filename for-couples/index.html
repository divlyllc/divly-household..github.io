The real problem

Why does one partner always end up managing the house?

1

The load is invisible

Noticing that the house needs something is work — but it never shows up anywhere. The partner who notices becomes the household manager by default.

2

Reminding feels like nagging

When one person holds the list, every request sounds like criticism. A shared system asks, so neither partner has to.

3

“Fair” is never measured

Both partners genuinely feel they do more, and there's no data either way. Divly makes the split visible, so the conversation starts from facts.

FAQ

Fairness questions, answered directly

How do partners split household tasks fairly?

The reliable way is to make all household work visible in one shared system, then track the split over time instead of arguing about single moments. Divly does this with shared tasks and habits plus daily rings and a Divly Score both partners can see.

What is the mental load in a relationship?

The mental load is the invisible work of noticing, planning and remembering household tasks — not just doing them. It typically falls on one partner. Divly reduces it by moving the noticing and planning into a shared app both partners see. More on why we built Divly.

Does Divly turn chores into a competition between partners?

No. The Divly Score measures the household, not a head-to-head contest. Rings and streaks are designed to make contributions visible and celebrate consistency together, not to keep score against each other.
